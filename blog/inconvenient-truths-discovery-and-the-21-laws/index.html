inconvenient truths, discovery, and the 21 laws

Aug 19 2021

The more I dive into learning and understanding product, the more I understand how important reading is. I’ve come across some incredible reads in the past week, some of which I’d like to put out here.

I’ve said incredible reads, I’m sure some of y’all must be thinking, “oh, Arvin read something by Marty Cagan ”. Damn right you are.

This article titled The Inconvenient Truth About Product speaks about the unavoidable, annoying, yet very real truth about building product. Marty sir says:

  • At least half of our ideas are not going to work
  • Even with ideas that prove to be valuable, useable, and feasible, it typically takes several iterations to get the implementation of the idea right

What I found really great about this read is its simplicity. There’s no beating around the bush, or a sense of false hope—just the inconvenient truth about product. Marty sir says that there is no escaping this—the real difference is how we deal with it. Here is where product discovery comes in.

Teresa Torres was the top result when I searched for product discovery on Youtube. This video opened up my mind to what discovery is about. Teresa ma’am starts off with what the lack of product discovery can lead to. She gives an example of the failure of Windows Me (2000)—a product that Microsoft worked very hard on and shipped, only to see no sales. What is funny is that Microsoft didn’t mess up anywhere during the build; they messed up before the build started. The product teams never found out whether customers really wanted a product like this. As the years passed, and with the introduction of Agile frameworks, product discovery has improved leaps and bounds. Today, when we think about product discovery, we are looking to answer questions like:

  • are we solving a problem customers care about?
  • will customers need/want/use this?
  • are we meeting stakeholder needs?
  • are we driving a desired outcome?

Apart from product, I’ve also started reading up on leadership and self-improvement. An insane book I found is The 21 Irrefutable Laws of Leadership by John C. Maxwell . This book blew my mind. Maxwell sir laid down 21 laws of leadership—21 fundamentals one must get right to succeed as a leader. Every law makes so much sense and has such great insight, I’ll be reading this one again and again, no question.

The law that was most game-changing to me was the last one: The Law of Legacy. It states that our abilities as leaders will not be measured by the buildings we built, the institutions we established, or what our team accomplished during our tenure. You and I will be judged by how well the people we invested in carried on after we are gone.

Maxwell sir asks “what do you want people to say at your funeral?”.
I’d say this line has really changed the way I think.

Peace out.
