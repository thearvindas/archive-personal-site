The Flawed Side of Design

Jul 5 2021

I’ve recently been studying how much of an impact design plays in our lives. Be it the apps we use, the goods we pick up at the supermarket, or listening to music on our headphones—good design is what paves for a good experience. Design is, inherently, what makes a product or a service worth coming back to.

In the Design of Everyday Things, Don Norman says, “Good design is actually a lot harder to notice than poor design, in part because good designs fit our needs so well that the design is invisible.”. Think about the times you’ve had a great experience using an app VS the time you had a frustrating one. Which one do you remember more vividly?

Clearly, design is crucial. But you’d be surprised how far from perfection we are when it comes to certain design principles.

A while back, I came across this particular tweet on Twitter, and it blew my mind.

So, what exactly is happening here? The Twitter algorithm appears to “favor” certain people’s faces based on skin tone, and hence, shows them as the photo preview. This leads us to question—was Twitter designed to be racist? What causes the algorithm to work this way?

An algorithm is simply a set of some logic that is making conclusions based on available data. Now, if all the data used is biased in a way, is it fair to believe that the algorithm itself won’t make biased decisions? No-fault of its own; it’s simply how it’s been trained. Consider a fruit recognition software—if I’m training my neural networks only on, say, apples, how can I expect a correct result when I test it on an orange or a banana? This is why diversity is critical in designing good products.

The Twitter incident isn’t a standalone one. Snapchat was in the spotlight for a while for making a filter that was insensitive to Asians. There was no intention of making a racist filter when this was designed—it is simply the result of a lack of diversity, hence improper and biased decisions. Consider voice recognition software. If you want this product to be used by people worldwide, you’ll have to design it for everyone. Inclusive and accessible design; sure, it understands an American accent but does it cater to everyone else?

What is our takeaway from this? It’s imperative to design for everyone. Make inclusive design. Inclusivity isn’t about using a diverse sample space or running every possible test case—it is the effort to want to build for everyone, to make your product something anyone can use and love. It’s not rocket science; it’s empathy. Make diversity a key component of anything you build. Always remember to keep design front and centered around the user—that’s how we build great products.
