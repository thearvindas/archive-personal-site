5w1h, user stories, and defining KPIs

Oct 8 2021

These are my notes from Solving Product Design Exercises by Artiom Dashinsky .

approaching a product design exercise#

Whenever you encounter a problem, don’t immediately dive into it and start thinking of solutions. The single most important thing to remember while solving product design exercises is to follow a process. Here are three tips to help you solve a problem:

  1. Make sure you understand the task: understand the deliverables, mode of delivery, fidelity level required, and the mode of presentation

  2. Ask questions and make assumptions: get all the required information, understand the task and goal, why this product must exist, who you’re building it for, and what problem it solves

    Ask clarifying questions to better understand the goal. Even after all these questions, you may not have all the information you need — you’ll have to make assumptions.

  3. Be critical about your solutions: always be prepared to reason out and justify your answers. You need to be aware of the “why” behind your design decisions. Since it is difficult to come up with the most perfect solution, being able to think critically and iterate on your solution is important

Solving a design exercise is basically designing a product in limited time and presenting your thinking process and the result. The 5W1H approach is a good way of bringing structure to your answer.

5W1H is a series of 6 questions:

  1. Why am I building this?
  2. Who am I building it for?
  3. When are where will it be used?
  4. What am I building?
  5. How could I measure it?

This is also what the framework (coming up next) is based on.

solving a product design exercise#

  1. Understand your goal (Why)
    The best way to start your solution is to define what you’re aiming for and how it helps the business. Think of the following aspects if you are:
    • building a new product:

    • Why is this product important?
    • What problem are we trying to solve?
    • What impact does it have on the world?
    • How does this product benefit users?
    • What business opportunities does it create?

    • improving an existing product:

    • Why is this product important?
    • Why does it need improvement?
    • What are we trying to improve?
    • What impact does it have on the world?
    • What business opportunities does it create?

  2. Define the audience (Who)
    The next step is to understand who you’re building the product for. Paul Graham, the founder of YCombinator says that the #1 reason startups/products fail is because it’s not something users want.

    Choosing the audience is very important, especially the high-level types of audience — people who’ll be your product’s primary users, who’ll shape and define your product.

    Once you choose the high-level audience, try understanding them better by describing them. Some important factors that can be considered are:

    • age
    • gender
    • location
    • occupation
    • mobility

    This is very important because a product is highly shaped by its users and their lives.

  3. Understand the customer’s context and needs (When and Where)
    Once the audience is defined, you need to identify when and where they experience a problem for which your product is a solution. Some questions that can be thought of:

    • where are they physically?
    • what triggers the need for a solution?
    • how much time do they have?
    • what emotions do they experience?

    Try understanding the users by mapping out user stories.
    As a [user], I want [goal/desire] so that [benefit].

    The problem can be further understood by mapping out the user journey and identifying pain points along the way.

  4. List ideas (What)
    Now that you have an idea of the problem and the users, you can start thinking of possible solutions. These are some properties that can be considered:

    • type of product (digital/physical)
    • platform (smartphone, TV, laptop, etc.)
    • type of interface (screen, graphics, audio, VR, AR, etc.)

    This is a template that can be used:
    Build X for [Who/Step 2], that [When and Where/Step 3] to [Why/Step 1]

  5. Prioritize and choose an idea
    Once you’ve explored the different approaches to solving the problem, choose the idea you believe is optimal. There is no perfect solution, so you’ll need to evaluate the upsides and downsides for all of them to evaluate the options. Here are a few considerations that will help weigh up each idea:

    • reach: how many customers would this solution reach and be able to help
    • value: which solution provides the highest value to the users
    • potential revenue: which solution meets the business goals of the company
    • implementation effort: which solution is the most feasible for us to build

    Plotting an impact-effort matrix is another way of picking a solution.

    • low impact + low effort: okay
    • low impact + high effort: bad
    • high impact + low effort: great
    • high impact + high effort: good

    Pick solutions that fall in the good/great region.

  6. Solve
    Now that the solution has been decided, we need to start figuring out implementation. This step includes a lot sketching — be it wireframes, user journeys, storyboarding, or any other ideas.

  7. Measure success
    Coming up with a solution is no good if you don’t know how to measure its impact/success. To measure success, we define Key Performance Indicators — quantifiable measures of performance over time for a specific objective. Some examples of metrics that could be measured:

    • task success rate
    • task completion rate
    • engagement
    • retention
    • revenue
    • conversion
    • user acquisition
    • net promoter score (NPS)
